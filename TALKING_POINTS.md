# Talking Points — Packaging-Dunnage Category Review
**MSBX 5470 | Case 1 | ~5–8 minute video presentation**

Split across 4 speakers. Suggested time targets in brackets.

---

## SPEAKER 1 — Slides 1–3 (~1:45)
*Covers: Title/Intro, Executive Summary, Category Background*

### Slide 1 — Title [0:00–0:15]
> "Good [morning/afternoon]. We're here today to present our Packaging-Dunnage Category Review for Specialty Aluminum Can Manufacturing. We'll walk through what we found in the 2022 spend data, the market trends shaping this space, the risks we see, and our recommendations for where to go from here."

---

### Slide 2 — Executive Summary [0:15–0:55]
> "Let's start with the headline finding. Our supervisor estimated this category at roughly $2 million. The actual 2022 spend was $6.37 million — that's 3.2 times the estimate, and a $4.37 million gap."

> "Across 33 transactions and 25 unique suppliers, this category represents about 4.85% of total company spend. That's not a small number, and given the supply challenges in this space, it deserves immediate strategic attention."

> "Three things stood out to us right away. First, our top three sub-categories — Layer Pads, End Bags, and Wood Pallets — represent 60% of spend, and all three are facing active supply disruption. Second, we have two single-source suppliers with no identified backup, covering $2.33 million of spend. That's a significant continuity risk. And third, our 25 US locations are spending far less per location than our South American sites — something that warrants investigation."

---

### Slide 3 — Category Background [0:55–1:45]
> "For context on what this category actually covers — we buy 10 types of packaging and dunnage materials. Layer Pads are the largest item at 30% of spend, followed by End Bags at 17%, and Wood Pallets at 14.5%. These materials are essential — they're how every aluminum can we produce gets shipped safely to customers."

> "The company operates 48 locations globally — 25 in North and Central America, 15 in Europe, and 8 in South America. And right now, all three regions are dealing with something. South America is experiencing severe inflation. Europe is navigating an energy crisis. And our US sites had two locations with significant workforce turnover in 2022, which creates demand variability and procurement process risk."

> "The other piece of context worth flagging: the supervisor estimated $2M. Actual spend was $6.37M. That gap tells us this category hasn't had the visibility it deserves — and that's exactly what this review is designed to fix."

---

## SPEAKER 2 — Slides 4–6 (~1:45)
*Covers: Sub-Category Spend, Geography, Supplier Pareto*

### Slide 4 — Sub-Category Breakdown [1:45–2:25]
> "Looking at how spend breaks down by sub-category — Layer Pads alone are nearly $1.9 million, about 30% of the total. Add in End Bags and Wood Pallets, and you're already at 60% of the category in just three line items."

> "What makes this significant is that those three sub-categories are also the ones facing the most active supply disruption. Layer pads and top frames have lead times of 8 to 16 weeks. Wood pallet prices are tied to volatile lumber markets. And stretch wrap — our fourth-largest sub-category at $867K — is sourced from a single supplier with no backup."

> "The implication is clear: our biggest spend items are also our biggest supply risks. That's not a coincidence — it reflects how critical these materials are — but it does mean we can't afford to have all of our eggs in one basket."

---

### Slide 5 — Geographic Breakdown [2:25–3:00]
> "Geographically, EMEA dominates at 52.5% of spend — $3.35 million. South America is second at 22.7%, and NCA comes in at 17.9%."

> "One number that stood out to us is spend per location. South America averages $180,000 per location across 8 sites. NCA averages just $45,700 per location across 25 sites. That's a 75% gap per location, and it doesn't make intuitive sense. Either NCA plants are buying packaging through channels that aren't captured in this dataset — which is a maverick spend problem — or there's a data coding issue. Either way, it needs to be investigated."

> "EMEA is also worth watching closely. One of our European locations is already experiencing energy disruptions, and our suppliers in that region are energy-intensive manufacturers. Cost pass-throughs are likely if the situation continues."

---

### Slide 6 — Supplier Pareto [3:00–3:30]
> "Now to the supplier story — and this is where I want to address something that might seem counterintuitive. We have 25 suppliers. That sounds like healthy diversity. But when you look at the spend distribution, our top 5 suppliers account for 72.9% of spend. Our top 6 hit 80%. The remaining 15 suppliers — 60% of our supplier base — share only 5.8% of spend."

> "That is not diversity. That is concentration. And within that concentration, we have two single-source situations: Angleboard for Layer Pads at $1.46 million, and Group O for Stretch Wrap at $867K. No backup. No alternative. If either of those suppliers has a disruption — which is entirely plausible given current market conditions — we have a production problem."

> "The silver lining is that Orbis and Rehrig, suppliers 5 and 8, both offer returnable packaging programs that we're not currently fully leveraging. That's an opportunity we'll come back to in recommendations."

## SPEAKER 3 — Slides 7–8 (~1:45)
*Covers: Market Research Findings, Risk Analysis*

### Slide 7 — Market Research Findings [3:30–4:25]
> "We identified 10 trends that will shape this category over the next 2 to 3 years. Let me walk through the highest-impact ones."

> "First, supply chain disruptions. This isn't a future risk — it's happening now. Lead times of 8 to 16 weeks for pallets, top frames, and layer pads are the reality today. Port congestion and logistics bottlenecks remain a persistent issue post-COVID, and the categories most exposed are exactly the ones where we have the most spend."

> "Second, raw material price volatility. Wood pallet costs are tied to softwood lumber markets, which have been extremely volatile. Stretch wrap and plastic pallets are tied to polypropylene and polyethylene resin — both driven by oil prices. We currently have no contractual protection against these swings."

> "Third, South American inflation. We have $1.44 million of spend in South America. Argentina alone saw inflation above 90% in 2022. The spend we see in the data is likely understated in real terms, and the situation may be worse in 2023."

> "On the medium-impact side — the EU Packaging and Packaging Waste Regulation, or PPWR, is coming. It mandates recycled content and reuse targets. For a company with 15 European locations, compliance costs and material substitutions are a real planning consideration."

> "And on the opportunity side — returnable packaging and global volume aggregation. Both are areas where we have existing supplier relationships and the scale to make a meaningful move."

---

### Slide 8 — Risk Analysis [4:25–5:15]
> "We formalized those findings into a risk register with six active risks."

> "The highest priority — rated High Impact, Medium Likelihood — is our single-source supplier exposure. $2.33 million sitting with two suppliers and no backup. In a market with 8 to 16 week lead times, that's not a theoretical risk. It's a real one."

> "Next are two financial risks that are both High Impact and High Likelihood right now: South American inflation and commodity price volatility. These aren't hypothetical — they are actively affecting our cost base. The issue is we have no contractual mechanism to manage them."

> "We also flagged EMEA energy disruption as High Impact and Medium Likelihood. One site is already experiencing disruptions. If that spreads or if energy surcharges become standard in our EU supplier contracts, the impact on our $3.35M EMEA spend could be material."

> "The data gap and NCA maverick spend risk is rated Medium Impact, but High Likelihood — because it's already reflected in the numbers. And finally, EU PPWR regulatory compliance is a medium-term risk that requires planning now, not after the regulations take effect."

> "Each of these has a specific mitigation strategy, which feeds directly into our recommendations."

## SPEAKER 4 — Slides 9–10 (~1:45)
*Covers: Stakeholder Questions, Recommendations & Close*

### Slide 9 — Stakeholder Interview Questions [5:15–6:00]
> "Before we move to recommendations, we want to be clear that this spend analysis is the starting point — not the full picture. To truly understand this category, we need to talk to the people living it every day. We've organized 12 interview questions across three functional groups."

> "With Operations and Supply Chain, we want to understand the real-world severity of the lead time problem. Are plants actually shutting down, or just cutting it close? Are we safety-stocking? And critically — are we getting the full value from our returnable packaging programs with Orbis and Rehrig?"

> "With Procurement and Commercial, the key question is: when did we last run a competitive bid for any of our top 5 suppliers? If the answer is 'I don't know' or 'several years ago,' that's a significant missed opportunity. We also want to understand whether the NCA spend gap represents real maverick spend or a data issue."

> "And with Finance and Strategy, we need to understand how packaging inflation is flowing through the P&L. Are we absorbing it or passing it through? Have we budgeted for PPWR compliance? And are our supplier specs being reviewed regularly for cost optimization, or are we just renewing the same specs year after year?"

> "These questions are designed to validate our findings, surface risks we can't see in the data, and build the stakeholder relationships we need to execute on these recommendations."

---

### Slide 10 — Recommendations & Close [6:00–7:15]
> "Our five recommendations are sequenced by urgency and impact."

> "Recommendation one is immediate: dual-source our two single-source suppliers. Angleboard for Layer Pads and Group O for Stretch Wrap represent $2.33 million of unprotected spend. We recommend issuing a mini-RFQ to two or three qualified alternatives per category by end of Q1. This is the single most important action we can take to reduce supply continuity risk."

> "Recommendation two: run competitive sourcing events for our top five suppliers. Even a 5% cost reduction on the top five — which is $4.71 million in combined spend — yields $235,000 in annual savings. A 10% reduction gets to $471,000. Given that we don't know when these suppliers were last competitively bid, this is likely low-hanging fruit."

> "Recommendation three: formally evaluate the total cost of ownership of a returnable packaging program with Orbis and Rehrig. Combined, they're at $989,000 of spend. Industry benchmarks suggest 15 to 30% TCO savings versus single-use pallets. We recommend a formal business case in Q2 2023."

> "Recommendation four: add commodity escalation and de-escalation clauses to our contracts, tied to published lumber and resin price indices. And simultaneously, audit NCA plant spending to understand the $45,000 per location gap."

> "And recommendation five is the longer-term strategic play: launch a global volume aggregation strategy. We have $6.37 million of spend across 48 locations. If suppliers like Orbis and Rehrig are present in multiple regions, a global master agreement could drive meaningful pricing improvements and standardized terms."

> "To summarize: we came in expecting a $2 million category. We found a $6.37 million category with significant supply concentration, commodity exposure, and geographic data gaps — and real opportunities to reduce cost and risk. We believe these five recommendations, if executed, could protect $2.33 million in at-risk spend and generate $583,000 to $1.37 million in annual savings and TCO reduction."

> "We're happy to take questions. Thank you."
